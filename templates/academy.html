Module I

The Macroeconomics of Inflation

Holding fiat currency is a mathematically guaranteed loss of purchasing power. Central banks systematically inject liquidity, decreasing the value of every rupee in circulation.

Investing is not an optional risk for wealth creation — it is a mandatory defensive mechanism to preserve what you already have. A savings account at 3% yield during 6% inflation yields a net -3% real return.

Fisher Equation: Real Return ≈ Nominal Return − Inflation Rate
# 10% BSE return during 6% CPI inflation → only 4% real purchasing power gain
# 3% FD during 6% CPI → -3% real return (you are losing money in real terms)
Module II

Market Mechanics & Valuations

A share of stock is a claim on a fraction of a company's future Free Cash Flows (FCF). The price you pay is a vote of confidence in those future earnings — nothing more.

Order Books & Liquidity Prices are set by the intersection of Bid (buyers) and Ask (sellers) orders. Every market move is the result of a real human or algorithm physically consuming liquidity from the order book.
P/E Ratio & Intrinsic Value Price-to-Earnings tells you how many years of current earnings the market is willing to pay. A P/E of 30 means investors expect 30 years of earnings at current rates — or strong future growth. It is not a price tag; it is a growth expectation contract.

Module III

Behavioral Finance & Cognitive Biases

The mathematics of investing is simple. The psychology is what destroys 90% of retail investors. Your brain is literally wired to make terrible investment decisions.

Loss Aversion (Kahneman)

The emotional pain of losing ₹1,00,000 is psychologically 2× more intense than the joy of gaining ₹1,00,000. This causes investors to panic sell structurally sound assets at market lows, permanently destroying capital.

Confirmation Bias

Investors actively seek out news articles and opinions confirming what they already believe, while unconsciously filtering out contradictory earnings data. The market has no bias — it processes all information equally and mercilessly.

Anchoring

When you buy a stock at ₹500 and it drops to ₹300, you become psychologically anchored to your purchase price. This "I'll sell when it gets back to ₹500" thinking ignores whether the fundamentals have structurally changed.

Herd Mentality

When everyone is buying (euphoria), you feel compelled to join. When everyone is selling (panic), fear forces you out. Institutional investors systematically profit from this predictable retail behavior — buying fear, selling greed.

Module IV

Modern Portfolio Theory & The Efficient Frontier

Harry Markowitz proved mathematically in 1952 that you can reduce portfolio risk without sacrificing return — simply by combining assets whose prices don't move in perfect sync. This is the mathematical foundation of all institutional money management.

The Sharpe Ratio

Measures return earned per unit of risk taken. A Sharpe > 1 is considered good. A Sharpe > 2 is exceptional. The formula: (Rp − Rf) / σp where Rf is the risk-free rate (G-Sec yield).

Correlation & Diversification

Assets with correlation = -1 perfectly cancel each other's risk. Gold and equities during market crashes tend toward negative correlation, which is why every institutional portfolio holds both.

Systematic vs Unsystematic Risk

Systematic risk (market risk, macroeconomic shocks) cannot be diversified away. Unsystematic risk (company-specific risk) is entirely eliminated through diversification — holding 20+ stocks eliminates ~95% of it.
